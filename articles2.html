How to Prepare for Competitive Programming (USACO)

March 14th 2022

By Eric Liang

As more and more people get into computer science, one of the ways many of these students learn about programming algorithms is through coding competitions, which allow you to practice both theoretical and practical applications of computer science. Many of these competitions, such as USACO and Codeforces, have different divisions for skill levels, and those at higher divisions are often sought out by colleges and companies alike. As someone who has been participating in these competitions for a couple years and currently in USACO Platinum, I’d like to share a couple tips for competitive programming.

How to Study

You obviously need a very solid foundational knowledge for competitive programming, including data structures and algorithms. There’s a lot of online resources and classes to learn these things, but the best free resource by far is usaco.org, which compiles dozens of different topics and sorts them. They do a very good job of explaining the ideas and even lists practice problems for you to do, which is incredibly important. I highly recommend committing time to these online resources and getting a good understanding of these ideas, since most coding competitions test you on your flexibility and adaptability with these ideas.

After each competition, you can further hone your skills by looking at the solution and determining which algorithm was needed to solve the problem. Be sure to understand that solution to the point where you can solve most similar problems, since coding competitions often like to repeat similar ideas. This also helps with any new topics you come across.

Other than the basic knowledge, even a simple Google search will show that the best way to study for competitive programming is to practice past problems, which is probably the only universal strategy that will work for everyone. Practicing past problems is incredibly important because it shows you the structure of how these competitions work and also what you can expect in them, such as how USACO problems are usually about optimization and working with large input data.

However, these competitions usually take hours to complete a single competition so it’s inefficient to blindly hack away at difficult problems, so it’s also important to maximize the gains from each individual problem.

One way to do so is by finding problems that are suited for your specific skill level, since easy problems won’t help you learn new topics and doing difficult problems are hard to understand and comprehend. This means you should usually only do problems of a single division and switch to harder problems when you feel like you’ve mastered that division. Codeforces is almost perfect for this because of how frequent their competitions are and how their point system works. This means their problems are often perfect for your current skill level and adjusts over time.

The Actual Test

Many programming competitions give the competitors a couple hours to solve multiple problems. These problems are all usually quite difficult and require a decent chunk of time (30 minutes to 90 minutes) so it’s important to delegate time properly during the actual test.

It’s crucial to start by reading all the problems because this gives you an understanding of their relative difficulty and creates a structure for the rest of the test. Perhaps there’s one problem that seems familiar or easy, in which case you should definitely do it first. However, if none of the questions stand out, you also shouldn’t be discouraged or give up since you still have plenty of time to brainstorm.

Speaking of brainstorming, a majority of your time will be spent trying to come up with a solution that works, rather than implementing the solution which doesn’t take that much time. Finding a good solution is better than finding a bad solution and debugging it since there might be several edge cases you fail to consider, and there’s even the chance that your solution doesn’t even work.

Since most competitions are about finding a solution that runs in under a certain time, be sure your solution has a passable time complexity or go back to the drawing board. You should ideally use a paper and pencil and play around with the problem until something really jumps out at you.

In the case that you don’t have enough time, some problems give you the opportunity to solve a subproblem with smaller test cases for slightly more points. There’s an argument surrounding whether you should tackle these problems first or last, but I like to always start by making a simpler solution for the subproblem and seeing if I can branch out to solve the larger problem. These subproblems often have a similar solution, so some minor optimizations would greatly help.

Conclusion

Finding a good way to study and optimizing your time on the test is undoubtedly a huge benefit for many competitive programmers, but the plain hard truth is to constantly grind away at past problems to truly understand how to solve these problems. You might get lucky with problems in a couple contests, but there’s no real way to “cheat” the system and be promoted to higher divisions. With this, I wish you the best of luck in your programming adventure and remember that competitive programming is only a small portion of the greater computer science world.
